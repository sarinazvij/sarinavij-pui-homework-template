History of Coffee


The oldest coffee plants have been cultivated by humans for hundreds of years. However, the origins of coffee as we know it today are still undiscovered. However, in the 1400s, coffee was being grown and traded in East Africa and the Arabian Peninsula. The desire to stay awake was the prime motivation for coffee consumption (much as it is the case today). In 1475, Constantinople became home to the first coffee shop. Quickly, drinking coffee became more of a social activity, often accompanied by performances or game-playing. When the practice of coffee-drinking spread to Europe in the 1600s, coffee became synonymous with wealth. Soon, only white men were permitted in coffee shops in England, and the culture associated with coffee began to experience a shift. At the start of the American Revolution, when America began protesting the British tax on tea, coffee rose to prominence. Due to Europe\'\'s inhospitable winter climate, coffee-production moved primarily to Central America. Today, Brazil cultivates the most coffee, followed by Vietnam.

Coffee Species


There are over 100 different species of coffee beans. However, these four dominate the global market today:

  • Arabica
  • Excelsa
  • Liberica
  • Robusta
The reason for this is because of their higher caffeine content and relative ease of growth compared to most other varieties.
